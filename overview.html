Background

In the realm of data analysis, simplification of a problem is crucial in order to extract meaningful patterns that can help answer pertinent questions. At times, it may be necessary to evaluate the entirety of the data set and differentiate unique groups based on their distinguishing characteristics. Conversely, in other instances, there may be a need to focus on specific groups in order to isolate the factors that render them distinctive. Clustering, as a sophisticated analytical technique, is instrumental in uncovering intricate patterns within complex data. It facilitates the identification of a comprehensive structure that offers insights into individuals within a dataset and carries significant policy implications. The primary objective of clustering is to cluster data units in a manner that underscores their "natural association" within the same cluster, while simultaneously exhibiting "distinctiveness" from other clusters (Anderberg, 1973).

Categorization, a fundamental and ubiquitous activity among human beings, involves the sorting of elements into distinct groups. The abundance of events we experience on a daily basis makes it impractical to process the unique properties of each individual stimulus without the use of categorical frameworks. In the absence of such schemes, every moment of the day would demand an equal amount of attention and cognitive resources, making it unfeasible to manage.

Classification is the process of assigning an item into a known category. Once a category is established, the essential attributes of the group are known and other items can be classified into that group. However, there may be some uncertainty with assigning any given observation. The complexity of the classification problem can arise due to imperfect definitions of classes, overlapping categories, and random fluctuations in observations. One way to deal with this is to find the probability the new item's membership with each category and classify according to some criterion (Anderberg, 1973). The most simple criterion is to classify based on the most probable category, but more sophisticated rules may be needed if the categories are not equally likely. Cluster analysis refers to discovering a category structure that fits the observations. Nothing is known about the category structure. The objective is to classify new observations such that "natural groups" form.

It is important to recognize that the process of categorization means that all qualities of the group, positive and negative, can potentially be assigned to each individual in the group. This can lead to certain feelings being associated with each group that may or may not be true for every individual. For example, a football player elicits a different emotional response than a priest. One must be careful of the potential of stereotypes. Every individual in each group is not exactly the same. There is a genetic explanation that we all possess unique qualities for the sake of diversity and survival of our species. Without our indiviudal characteristics, we might not be different from a society of robots.

The analyst of the clustering problem is usually informed about the desirable properties of category structures.

The reason clustering is so difficult is because of the large number of ways that n observations can be sorted into m groups. Abramowitz & Stegun (2014) show the mathematical formulation of this quantity, which reaches 2,436,684,974,110,751 if we have 25 observations and 5 groups. This problem gets compounded due to the fact that the number of groups is unknown. It would take a long period of time to examine all of the possible groups and we would lose the ability to make meaningful distinctions between scenarios. Thus, clustering algorithms were created to try to solve this problem. In our final project, we examine three such algorithms, known as k-means, k-means ++, and DBSCAN. Each of the clustering tabs desribes the algorithm used, and shows a visual representation of how each algorithm is performed. The comparison tab shows how each algorithm compares to one another at each step.

The visual representation of clustering becomes limited as one goes beyond two dimensions. While, some packages allow for a visual of a 3 dimensional clustering result, we cannot visualize the fourth dimension well. So, there are visual limitations as the clustering is done on multiple categories.
